The problem

A humanoid is a pendulum that must not fall.

Standing on a small foot, the robot balances its mass above a narrow base. The unstable part of that dynamics is a single scalar — the divergent component of motion, the capture point. Balance is exactly the task of keeping it over the support. A push, noisy contact sensing, and the geometry of a 17 cm foot conspire against you: when the capture point leaves the support, the robot either steps to catch it, or falls.

The system

Three layers, one real-time loop.

A whole-body nonlinear MPC plans the motion; a momentum observer infers the unmodelled push; an active layer feeds that estimate forward and, when needed, commands a step. All of it closes the loop on the physical robot at hardware rates.

01

Centroidal NMPC

35-state model (centroidal momentum + whole-body kinematics), 1.2 s horizon, transcribed by multiple shooting and solved with SQP on HPIPM. Replans at 80 Hz.

02

6-D wrench observer

A cascaded centroidal-momentum observer reconstructs the external wrench from measured momentum and contact forces — a triple pole that detects a push in ~24 ms.

03

Active rejection

The estimate is fed forward into the momentum-rate equation; when the capture point still escapes, a capturability-triggered step is committed. Together they roughly double the push envelope.

The elegance

One constant, one saturation.

Project the closed loop onto the horizontal plane and it collapses to a linear inverted pendulum with a single time constant, ω = √(g/h) = 3.52 rad/s. Three results follow from that one number and the geometry of the foot — and each is something you just felt in the sandbox.

saddle

Why simulation isn't deterministic.

The pendulum is a saddle: eigenvalues ±ω. The divergent mode has a finite-time Lyapunov exponent of exactly +ω, so on the recovery boundary any seed — scheduling jitter, finite-precision — grows like e+ωt and flips the outcome. Deep inside, runs reproduce.

critical regions

Why the observer beats the oracle.

The feedforward enters the QP only through the constraint right-hand side: the solution is piecewise-affine in the estimate. Push hard and the CoP hits the forefoot facet — a critical-region boundary. The oracle's full-magnitude command over-commits past it; the band-limited observer stays inside, and recovers further.

level crossings

Why the step trigger has a noise floor.

The step fires when a monitored signal crosses a level. Under noise, false crossings follow a Kac–Rice floor ν+ = (ΩR/2π)e−z²/2, set by the signal's spectral width — not by ω. A filtered-velocity gate raises the margin and collapses that rate.

Sensitive dependence on the recovery boundary

The divergent coordinate is the capture point ξ, with ξ̇ = ω·ξ — a pure +ω mode. Launch a fan of near-identical seeds: deep inside the boundary they all recover and runs reproduce; on the line ξ = x_front the seed is amplified past the foot edge and the outcome becomes a coin-flip.

critical regions · live

Band-limiting keeps the feedforward off the facet

The feedforward enters the QP only through the constraint right-hand side, so the control is piecewise-affine in it. The cascaded observer band-limits a sharp push into a lower-peak estimate — and a lower peak stays in the productive region instead of over-committing past the foot facet.

level crossings · live

The step trigger has a Kac–Rice noise floor

Under noise the monitored signal crosses the trigger level at a rate ν⁺ = (Ω_R/2π)·e−z²/2, set by the margin in standard deviations z = u/σ. Raise the margin — what the filtered-velocity gate does — and the floor collapses exponentially.

The evidence

Recovery, measured.

Closed-loop on the full robot in simulation. Frame-by-frame: the same disturbance fails without rejection, recovers in place with the observer, and is caught by a step when it is too large to absorb.
